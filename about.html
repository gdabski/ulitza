ulitza street names

What is ulitza

A view into each country's unique history as told by its most famous heroes, leaders and poets. How are there warriors telling their history in our case? Simple. We find out how each country has chosen to name their own streets, squares, public institutions and similar landmarks. Among these, we only select the ones designating a person name. In short, eponyms. We then let each country boast its own list of heroes that have shaped its history. All entries have wikipedia links, so we can explore in depth.

In some of the cases, the names reveal strong nationalistic tendencies, where the fight for independence plays the leading role. In other cases, they bear the scars of outside influences, where strong empires have culturally eclipsed its neighbours or colonial powers have spread their influence far and wide.

The homepage is a list of persons sorted by the number of countries where they appear at least once.

Etymology

From the Romanian uliță, meaning a narrow road, used most frequently for streets in small villages borderd on both sides by houses, in contradiction to the city boulevards, for example. Originally a proto-slavic word, ulita, it has many variants in most of the Eastern-Europeean countries and languages,

Aromanian: ulitsã
Belarus: вуліца (vúlica)
Bosnian: ulica
Bulgarian: у́лица (úlica)
Croatia: ulica
Czech: ulice, ulicě
Hungarian: utca
Romanian: uliță
Polish: ulica
Slovak: ulica
Russian: у́лица (úlica)
Slovene: úlica
Ukrainian: у́лиця (úlycja), ву́лиця (vúlycja)

Method and data source

All data is taken from openstreetmap.

All wikipedia links are to the English pages, if it exists. If not, to the language of the country where the eponym appears in. The display name is based on the wikipedia entry, and not on the name as it actually appears on the streets and landmarks. You can check the github page for each country to get the raw info.
